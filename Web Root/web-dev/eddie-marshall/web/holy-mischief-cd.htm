Eddie Marshall Holy Mischief

Buy from Amazon

Buy from Eddie »

This long-awaited CD was released at the beginning on September 1999 on Eddie's own label, Ruddy Duck Records. The group, called "Holy Mischief, consists of Paul Nagel on piano, Jeff Chambers on bass, Kenny Brooks on saxophone, and Eddie on drums and recorder. Jeff Cressman played trombone on "Wildwood."

  1. Holy Mischief (7 minutes, 12 seconds)
  2. Monsieur de Charles (4 minutes, 2 seconds)
  3. Remember When (7 minutes, 43 seconds)
  4. Luna Nueva (6 minutes, 15 seconds)
  5. Sue Jeanius (5 minutes, 12 seconds)
  6. Dreams That Dance (4 minutes, 47 seconds)
  7. Too Much Romance (4 minutes, 26 seconds)
  8. The Bridges of Terrell County (4 minutes, 39 seconds)
  9. Wildwood (7 minutes, 48 seconds)

Liner Notes

"The joy is being able to play this music and relax."

That's Eddie Marshall's pronouncement on jazz and drumming; but he could extend it to other of his life's choices, including his longtime residence in San Francisco. He first visited the city over forty years ago with Toshiko Akiyoshi, who like Bobby Hutcherson and Bobby McFerrin (and many others), have counted on Eddie as a consummate sideman.

One of Eddie's declared missions on Holy Mischief is to give accolades to West Coast musicians", ably represented here by pianist Paul Nagel's "post-Bebop thing", by bassist Jeff Chambers' tonal support and confident timing, and in Kenny Brooks' contemporary sound and concept which is 'more like post-Trane'. Jeff Cressman serves double duty: as Holy Mishchief's sound engineer and as the haunting trombonist on "Wildwood", written by Eddie for his late stepson. Eddie himself doubles, on "Dreams That Dance" and "Monsieur Charles," with his alto recorder, the Baroque instrument to which he returned with his doctor's blessing after cardiac surgery in 1984.

But Eddie isn't likely to abandon his dynamic reputation as a drummer and this album has a mission of time, as well as place. "What I am really experimenting with," says the leader, "is doing odd times, like nine, seven, six, or five, but without accenting the 'one', and that's the difficult part." With characteristic modesty, he understates the difficulty of his achievement., because there are few other jazz drummers who can innovate with meter, while mixing solid propulsion and spontaneous combustion, all without pretense or pushiness.

— from Holy Mischeif CD liner notes written by Jeff Kaliss
