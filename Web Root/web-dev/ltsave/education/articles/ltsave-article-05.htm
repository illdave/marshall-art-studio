Mutual Funds I: What They Are And Why You Should Consider Investing In Them

Should you own just one good stock or bond, or should you own a portfolio of many good stocks and bonds? As you have probably guessed or assumed, it is better to own a portfolio, for reasons that we explain further in our introductions to asset class allocation and risk. You could put together your own portfolio, of course, by buying stocks and bonds by yourself. Many individuals do. But consider, first, if you can spare the time to take up the new hobby of investment analysis. You can? Do you think you know enough to do as well or better than the professionals? Maybe you should hire a professional instead. But if your net worth doesn't rank you among the upper reaches of American society, you probably can't afford to hire a professional manager to build and maintain your own appropriate portfolio. Mutual funds, however, provide a way for many investors -- usually small investors -- to pool their money and hire a professional manager.

The most common form of mutual fund is what is called an "open-end" fund, which is just such a pool. Every open-end mutual fund is organized as a private company. The money that you invest is a contribution to the pool, and you own shares in proportion to your investment in the total. The company has trustees who meet regularly to look after the investors' interests, including the hiring of a manager. The manager buys and sells securities - stocks, bonds, even sometimes shares in other mutual funds. The fund prospectus spells out what kinds of investments the manager is allowed to make, and you should read carefully the relevant paragraphs in the prospectus.

The value of a mutual fund, of course, reflects the value of the investments that it holds. If the fund holds stocks, for example, and the stocks' prices go up, then the value of the fund goes up, along with your investment in the fund. At the end of every business day, the company that manages the fund determines the current price of every security that the fund holds, adds these up to find the total value of the fund's pool of investments, and divides this by the number of shares that investors hold. This result is the value of one share of the fund, and is called the Net Asset Value, or NAV; it is published the next day in the newspapers. The fund manager is required by law to do this, and to do it accurately. You can easily work out the value of your investment if you keep track of the number of shares you own. Just multiply the number of shares by the NAV, and you'll know the value of your investment.
