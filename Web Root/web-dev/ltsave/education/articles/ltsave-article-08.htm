The Uncertain Nature of Returns

It is fairly easy to predict the returns of less risky assets. The whole point of calling them "less risky", after all, is that there isn't much uncertainty about what they will return.

The future returns of risky assets, however, are uncertain. No one knows (though many try to guess) what the stock market will return next year, or even what the long-term average return will be.

We can get a sense of the range of possibilities, however, by looking to history as a guide. The following table illustrates the history of returns since 1900:


 Average Best Year Worst Year Worst Decade Best Decade
Stocks 12.0 57.6 -43.9 -0.1 17.9
Bonds 5.1 40.4 -9.2 0.0 12.3
Cash 4.1 14.7 0.0 0.7 8.7

It is also instructive to look at returns decade by decade:


 00s 10s 20s 30s 40s 50s 60s 70s 80s 90s
Stocks 10.2 4.7 13.4 -0.1 9.4 17.9 8.1 6.7 16.1 14.4
Bonds 2.7 2.7 6.0 5.1 3.3 0.0 1.5 5.7 12.3 9.7
Cash 4.7 4.8 4.8 0.7 0.7 1.9 3.8 6.4 8.7 5.0

These numbers, while giving us a sense of the range of possibilities, do not, in themselves, point to any distinct forecast. Will the 2000's be a repeat of the '90s or of the '30s? No one knows. In fact no one knows if the 2000's will even be within the historical ranges. Maybe new ground will be broken.

Nonetheless, history is the starting point for forecasting returns. The simplest way to apply the range of past returns to the forecasting of future possibilities is to assume that in any given future year, each of the past years is equally likely to repeat itself. Usually, however, it is possible to improve upon this naïve approach by taking into account the level of interest rates, dividend yields, economic growth, and the like. The end product of this process is a description of all possible returns, with relative likelihoods assigned to each one. Such a description is called a probability distribution.

A probability distribution is typically summarized by two statistics: its expected value (the average of all possibilities) and its standard deviation (a measure of how different the range of possibilities is from the average), which allows statisticians to calculate odds. For example, if the expected return on stocks is 10% and the standard deviation is 15%, then the odds are approximately two to one that the return in any given year will be between -5% and 25%, and the odds are approximately nineteen to one that returns will be between -20% and 40%.

While stock market returns in any given year are very uncertain, the average return over time is less uncertain. This is because the ups and downs of the market partially offset each other over time. Consider the example, given above, of a 10% expected return and a 15% standard deviation. With a 25-year investment horizon, the standard deviation of the average return will be 3%, which translates into odds of approximately two to one that the average return for 25 years will between 7% and 13%.

This does not mean - contrary to common belief - that the risk of the stock market falls the longer your time horizon. The difference between 7% and 13% is very large when compounded over 25 years. The case for a large equity allocation when you have a longer time horizon rests more solidly on the fact that longer-horizon investors are more likely to be risk tolerant, because they have more time to adapt to unfavorable developments.

In any case, when investing in risky assets, it is very important to remember that a range of returns is possible, and that a good plan will require adapting to future developments as they unfold.
