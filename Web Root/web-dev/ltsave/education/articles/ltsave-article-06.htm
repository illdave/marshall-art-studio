Mutual Funds II: Kinds Of Funds

Mutual funds can be classified in several different ways that are not mutually exclusive. There are active and passive funds, there are style funds, and there are load funds and no-load funds.

Investment professionals have two broad philosophies of investing: active and passive management. An active manager actively researches each stock or bond under consideration and tries to buy the ones with the best prospects and to avoid or to sell the ones whose prospects appear poor. Most mutual fund managers are active managers (and, if they are active manager, they may then have different style philosophies, like investing in value stocks or growth stocks). A passive manager doesn't make judgments about a security's prospects, but instead puts together a portfolio that is intended to track a market index. The best-known index, apart from the Dow Jones Industrials, is the Standard and Poor's 500, but there are other market indices as well. For example, there are indices that track sectors of the bond market, or the stocks of foreign countries and regions. There are also indices that are supposed to represent the broad stock market even better than the S&P 500. Index funds are designed just to track an index, and the most popular index funds track the S&P 500. Many, perhaps most investment professionals believe strongly in either passive or active portfolio management, and some think that there is a place for both kinds of funds in an overall portfolio. We at LTSave believe that the arguments for passive management are very strong, but that the drawback to using index funds is that they necessarily do not reflect the particular and different needs of different investors.

LTSave believes that you should consider very carefully two aspects of any mutual fund into which you are thinking of putting your money. One is the style of the fund, and the other, which cover in another essay, is the cost of ownership of the fund. By "style," we mean, "Is it a small-cap. fund?" "Is it a bond fund?" "If it is a stock fund, is it a growth fund or is it a value fund?" In short, "What kind of fund is it?" When you can answer this question, you can determine whether the fund is right for you, and whether it belongs with other mutual funds in your financial portfolio. LTSave performs a rigorous style analysis to find out, first, if a fund can be classified, second, what category it can be classified into, and third, whether the fund manager is consistent in maintaining the fund's style.
