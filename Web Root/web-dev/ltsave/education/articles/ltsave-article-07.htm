Mutual Funds III: Costs Of Ownership

Cost of ownership is critical and is too easily overlooked by investors, perhaps because the cost can be very difficult to calculate. There are two main costs: the fees charged by the management company, and the taxes that you may have to pay on your funds' earnings. Let's consider fees. There are multiple fees that a management company may charge. This leads to another two-fold division of the world of mutual funds: there are load funds and no-load funds. The load, which is a percentage taken off the top when you invest (or a percentage off the total when you sell) is a fee paid to compensate the salesperson who sold you the fund. We at LTSave believe that, in most instances, whatever any load fund's other good qualities might be, the load is likely to add so much to the cost of ownership that you're best off avoiding load funds, and we do not generally recommend them. A load by another name is a 12b-1 fee; this refers an amount charged by the fund manager at regular intervals, for as long as you hold the fund, to compensate for the cost of the sales and marketing of the fund. This fee is capped at a relatively small percentage, but because it is charged every year for as long as you own the fund, its overall effect can significantly subtract from the value of your assets. The rest of the fees charged are compensation for the costs of running the fund, which includes not just salaries and research materials, but all other necessary items, including the accounting systems and the mailings that you receive.

The other major cost of ownership in a fund, taxes, is not a consideration for mutual funds that are held in 401(k) or other tax-advantaged retirement plans until you begin to withdraw funds during retirement. At that time, taxes may matter, but that depends upon the type of retirement plan and where the money goes. The money might, to mention two examples, be rolled over into an existing IRA, or it might be used to purchase an annuity. Each of these actions has its own tax consequences. At any rate, there is little point, when selecting mutual funds for a 401(k) plan, to choose funds that offer tax advantages. These advantages are intended for investors who purchase the funds for taxable accounts.
