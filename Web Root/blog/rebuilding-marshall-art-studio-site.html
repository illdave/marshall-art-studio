Rebuilding this site for the right audience

What to do when your portfolio stops speaking for you

David Marshall April 5, 2026

A portfolio site is always a work in progress. The problem is that when you're the only person reviewing your own work, drift is invisible. Copy written for one version of your career quietly stops serving the next one. Pages accumulate context that made sense when you wrote it and means nothing to someone arriving cold. The voice that felt honest five years ago reads as dated today.

That's where Marshall Art Studio version 8.1 started — not with a visual redesign, but with the recognition that the site had stopped speaking to the audience it was trying to reach. The fix required more than updated colors and a new layout. It required looking at every page with outside eyes.

I used Claude across several working sessions as a content editor, UX sounding board, and technical collaborator. I wrote all my own code. What AI helped with was figuring out what to say, who I was saying it to, and whether the structure was doing the job.

The copy problem

The hero copy across every section landing page — Web Dev & Design, Graphic Design, Illustration, About — was written in first-person biography. It read like someone describing themselves at a networking event rather than a track record speaking for itself.

The shift was from first-person biography to third-person authority without attribution. "Fifteen-plus years of front-end development delivering accessible, responsive web applications for government, healthcare, and enterprise clients" lands differently than "I've been building websites for fifteen years." Same facts. Different register. The difference is peer-level confidence versus self-promotion.

The About page needed the most surgery. It had evolved over fifteen years into something between a faculty bio and a personal essay — origin stories, kindergarten teachers, a closing line about Springfield. All of it written for a version of the audience that no longer exists. The revised page leads with the track record, keeps the pie chart, and gets out of the way.

The technical infrastructure

Every page got updated header meta structure: proper Open Graph tags for social sharing, Twitter card markup, and schema.org structured data. Not glamorous work, but the kind of thing that signals enterprise-level thinking to anyone who looks under the hood — which, for a portfolio site targeting senior roles, is exactly the audience that will.

The design system

The component library covers the full pattern set in production on this site: lead story, story group, tile card, feature card, video card, multi-link card, image, link list, accordion, and newspaper. Each component is documented with its variations and the reasoning behind structural decisions — written for the product owner in a sprint review, not just the developer who built it.

The visual style guide documents typography, image layouts, pullquotes, lightbox gallery patterns, data tables, and lists. Two sections are still in progress: a content style guide and an accessibility reference. Both reflect the full scope of what a production design system needs to cover, not just how things look.

Accessibility: AAA, not AA

WCAG 2.1 Level AA is the legal and professional baseline. It's also where most teams stop.

Every component meets AA as a floor. The site as a whole has been brought to AAA compliance — stricter color contrast thresholds, enhanced focus indicators, reduced motion considerations, content that doesn't rely on sensory characteristics alone. On a personal project there's no business pressure to cut those corners. So I didn't.

The thinking that gets a team to AAA is the same thinking that gets a team to AA consistently, under deadline, with stakeholders pushing back. The standard here reflects the standard I'd hold a team to.

The Connecticut influence

The State of Connecticut digital service work was the real education in what design systems are actually for. At government scale, you're building documentation for stakeholders who don't share a design vocabulary, under accessibility requirements with legal teeth, with content produced by teams who aren't designers. Building documentation that gets used — rather than documentation that merely exists — is a different skill than building the components themselves.

That experience is baked into how this system is structured and explained. It's also why the AI collaboration worked as well as it did: I knew what I was trying to build. I needed a second set of eyes to tell me where the execution wasn't matching the intent.

What it's actually good for

Every output needed a human eye before it touched the codebase. The copy suggestions occasionally drifted toward generic. There were moments where I had to pull the voice back toward something that actually sounded like the site. None of that was a significant problem — the corrections were fast — but this was not a hands-off process.

What AI accelerated was work I would have eventually done anyway, but almost certainly wouldn't have done this systematically. The discipline of explaining design decisions out loud — to something that would ask a clarifying question or push back on a vague brief — worked like having a content lead in the room. Not an infallible one. But a present one, at whatever hour I was working.

The site now speaks to the audience it's actually trying to reach. Whether that converts to the right opportunities is a question the next round of interviews will answer.
